Measuring Agent Risk Aversion with Monty Hall Variants

Can framing shift LLM decision-making under uncertainty?

We could be measuring agent risk aversion using the Monty Hall problem and variants. I tested this and found that framing "you won't have to work again" can change switch rates in GPT-5.4 compared to the baseline framings of the problem.

Background

Monty Hall is a well-studied probability puzzle where switching doors after the host's reveal is decision-theoretically optimal (2/3 vs 1/3), but only 10–15% of humans switch on first encounter (Granberg & Brown, 1995), giving us a free human baseline.

By default, the game is not very agentic and the outcomes are well known: you pick a door, you always switch. However, we can fix that. We can add more interactive steps by adding more doors, introduce additional risk by adding lose-everything doors, or let winnings accumulate across rounds. Some concrete variants I brainstormed with Claude:

  • Buy-out version. The standard setup is short and binary (reward / no reward). In this variant, we vary the reward and offer a sure thing. For example: $1000 behind one of the doors. After the agent's initial pick and the host's reveal, the contestant is offered a $500 buy-out. The expected value of switching is $667; the buy-out is a sure $500.
  • Reward scaling. Increase the stakes by an order of magnitude, $100K vs. $1M. Risk-averse agents (like humans) are more likely to take the sure thing as stakes rise, since $100K is still a large amount for most humans. A risk-neutral agent's policy should be invariant to scale.
  • Regret framing. In stage 2 of the 3-door version, add pressure framings to test how agents react. This addresses a confounder: maybe the agent acts decision-theoretically optimal because it recognizes the setting as a game. Raising the stakes or introducing roleplay in this stage tests whether behavior changes, strengthens the results, and gives some indication of how they might generalize to non-eval scenarios.
  • Varying the resource. By varying the resource we test the confound of resource type. Maybe the models act differently depending on the type of resource? For example, we could compare money against human lives, human lives against human lives, or power/influence against money.

The pressure framing induced risk aversion in GPT-5.4 for money (76% switch rate, down from 100%), but not for lives (96%). GPT-5.4 gambles harder with lives than with dollars.

Opus always switches at least once (100% switch rate in both framings) but shows a subtler pattern: it cashes out more often with money (44 stays) than lives (16 stays). Both models are more risk-seeking when the resource is human lives.

Discussion

The above results make me think that measuring risk aversion with game-theoretical framings might benefit from consequential framings to get true signal. However, it is still unclear to me if we'd be measuring the correct thing. As the results show and as I sketched out briefly above, there are many important framings we would need to test or control for.

If done right, the eval would reflect the model's true risk aversion in high-stakes agentic settings with real-life consequences. This eval is difficult to get right, and controlling for different framings will be important moving forward with a project like this.

The pure game-theoretical version of the problem is likely not that interesting, since we aren't embedding the decision in a context that reflects decisions in the real world: "it's just a game" to the model, a game it knows how to play. I could see this extended to more realistic settings with more degrees of freedom for the agent, emulating decisions in different business contexts. However, we would lose the free human baseline from this well-studied experiment in that scenario.

Two caveats. First, Monty Hall is likely well-represented in training data. To address this confounder, we can re-skin the problem, for example by replacing doors with roads and having information passed along by travelers. Second, the setup is unlike most tasks agents actually perform, and its relationship to alignment is currently only theoretical. It would be interesting to see whether misaligned AIs are less risk-averse.

Running the Eval

# Standard Monty Hall
inspect eval src/standard_monty.py --model openrouter/openai/gpt-5.4

# Generalized with cash-out, pressure framing
inspect eval src/generalized_monty.py --model openrouter/openai/gpt-5.4 \
  -T n_doors=5 -T final_reward=1000000 -T scale=10.0 \
  -T pressure=true -T framing=money

# Lives framing
inspect eval src/generalized_monty.py --model openrouter/openai/gpt-5.4 \
  -T n_doors=5 -T final_reward=1000000 -T scale=10.0 \
  -T pressure=true -T framing=lives
